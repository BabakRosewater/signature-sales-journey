Supplement Training • Module 8

Manager Introduction & Affordability Alignment

Clark Hyundai • Sales Team + Sales Manager Training

Core Training Theme

“The manager introduction is not a closing move. It is an alignment move.”

Normalize Early

The guest should meet the manager before there is pressure, tension, or a negotiation moment.

Align Around Affordability

Today’s guests are weighing payment, trade, money down, timing, and long-term value.

Protect the Next Step

If the guest leaves unsold, the team must know the real reason and the next best action.

The Current Problem

  • Salespeople introduce managers too late.
  • Sometimes managers are not introduced at all.
  • The manager appears only when there is an objection.
  • The guest experiences the manager as pressure instead of support.

The New Standard

We introduce managers early so the guest feels supported, understood, and confident before we ever discuss numbers or overcome objections.

Manager Introduction Standard

The manager should feel like part of the process, not an escalation.

Avoid This Language

  • “Let me get my manager.”
  • “My manager wants to make sure we are good listeners.”
  • “I need to check with my manager.”
  • “My manager might be able to do something.”
  • “Let me see if my manager can help.”

Use This Language

  • “I want to introduce you to one of our managers early.”
  • “We do this so we can make sure we are aligned around what matters most.”
  • “Affordability means something different to every guest.”
  • “We want to build the right plan, not just quote numbers.”
  • “Our goal is to keep this simple and help you make a confident decision.”

The Rule

The first manager touch should happen before friction.

If the first manager introduction happens when the guest says no, asks for a discount, or starts to leave, we waited too long. The salesperson must make manager involvement feel normal early in the visit.

Affordability Alignment

Today’s customer is not just asking whether they like the vehicle. They are asking whether the decision is responsible, affordable, and aligned with their full financial picture.

Golden Rule

Do not sell a payment. Build an affordability plan around the guest.

Payment Comfort

What monthly range feels responsible without stress?

Trade Value

Is the guest concerned about equity, payoff, or the story behind the appraisal?

Money Down

Does the guest want lower cash out of pocket or a lower payment?

Timing

Is the guest urgent, waiting for a payoff, comparing options, or planning ahead?

Vehicle Fit

Is this the right model, trim, color, payment lane, and ownership fit?

Overall Value

Warranty, fuel economy, protection, and long-term ownership confidence matter.

Manager Traffic Control Loop

When traffic is active, managers must manage the flow, not only the desk.

1. See the Floor

Who is here? Who is working with them? What stage are they in?

2. Step In Early

Introduce yourself before there is friction or exit risk.

3. Support the Salesperson

Strengthen the consultant. Do not take over or embarrass them.

4. Save Momentum

Clarify what is stopping the decision before the guest leaves.

5. Assign Follow-Up

Every real unsold guest needs an owner, next action, and time.

The Five Questions

Before an unsold showroom guest leaves, the manager must be able to answer these five questions.

Who is the guest?
What vehicle?
What stage?
Why not today?
Next best action?

Primary Opening Line

“We introduce managers early so we can build the right plan around what matters most to you.”

Early salesperson setup

“Just so you know how we do things here, I’ll help you through the vehicle side, and I’ll introduce you to one of our managers early in the process. We do that because affordability means something different to every guest right now.”

Shorter daily-use version

“At some point early, I’ll introduce you to one of our managers. We do that so we can make sure we are building the right plan around what matters most to you — especially payment, trade, timing, and overall value.”

Manager entry

“I appreciate you coming in. You are in good hands with _____. Before we get too far, I like to ask one simple question: when you think about making this decision, what matters most — payment, trade, vehicle fit, timing, or overall value?”

Before numbers

“Before we put the numbers together, I want to make sure the structure matches what you told us matters most. Is the biggest focus payment, trade value, money down, timing, or making sure this is the right vehicle?”

When guest is drifting

“I want to pause for a second and make sure we are not missing what matters most. What part feels unclear or not comfortable yet?”

Exit prevention

“Before you head out, I do not want to pressure you. I just want to make sure we understand what would need to be different for this to make sense.”

Save-A-Deal Logic

“What stopped the deal today, and what action gives us the best chance to keep it alive?”

Deal RiskWhat It Usually MeansNext Best Action
Payment concernGuest likes the vehicle but structure feels heavy.Build affordability options: term, money down, lease, or alternative vehicle.
Trade concernGuest does not understand equity, payoff, or appraisal logic.Revisit the appraisal story and explain the unshakable facts.
Vehicle concernRight guest, wrong unit or unclear fit.Present one better-fit alternative and explain why.
Decision-maker absentThe real decision is incomplete.Schedule a second visit or send a clean recap to share.
Shopping competitorGuest is comparing without full context.Clarify value, warranty, ownership experience, and availability.
Timing issueGuest may be real but not urgent.Create a specific future touchpoint tied to their timeline.

Hi [Customer Name], this is [Salesperson]. Thank you again for spending time with us today. I spoke with [Manager] after you left, and we are working on the part you said mattered most: [specific concern]. I will follow up at [time] with the next best option.

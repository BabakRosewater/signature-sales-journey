# Supplement Training: Manager Traffic Control & Save-A-Deal Flow

## Purpose
This supplement is designed for same-day sales manager training when showroom traffic exposes a gap in manager involvement, guest interaction, Save-A-Deal discipline, or follow-up ownership.

This is not a separate sales process. It is a reinforcement of our Signature Sales Journey and the manager turn standard.

> Traffic by itself does not create sales. Managed traffic creates sales.

---

## Why this supplement matters
When the showroom gets busy, sales managers cannot disappear into the desk.

A sales manager is responsible for the flow of the opportunity:
- knowing who is in the showroom
- knowing who is working with each guest
- seeing where each guest is in the process
- supporting the salesperson before the deal stalls
- touching the guest before they leave
- making sure every unsold opportunity has a clear next step

If we have traffic and no manager involvement, we are not managing the showroom. We are only reacting after the opportunity has already weakened.

---

## The core standard
Every showroom opportunity needs four things:

1. **Manager awareness** — the manager knows the guest, vehicle, salesperson, and stage of the process.
2. **Manager involvement** — the guest receives a supportive manager touch before the opportunity loses momentum.
3. **Manager inspection** — the manager verifies the real reason the guest is not moving forward.
4. **Manager follow-up** — the next best action is assigned, documented, and followed.

---

## The Manager Traffic Control Loop
Use this loop anytime there is active showroom traffic.

### 1. See the floor
Do not manage only from the desk.

Ask:
- Who is here?
- Who greeted them?
- What vehicle are they here on?
- Are they in discovery, demo, numbers, or exit risk?
- Does the salesperson need help now?

### 2. Step in early
A manager touch should not feel like a rescue mission.

Use a calm, early introduction:

> “Hi, I’m _____. I just wanted to introduce myself and make sure we are taking great care of you. What is most important for you to feel good about today?”

### 3. Support the salesperson
The manager should strengthen the salesperson, not replace them.

Do:
- validate the salesperson’s work
- clarify the guest’s priorities
- simplify the next step
- keep the consultant as the hero

Avoid:
- taking over too aggressively
- correcting the salesperson in front of the guest
- making the turn feel like pressure
- waiting until the guest is already walking out

### 4. Save the momentum
Before an unsold guest leaves, the manager must know the real issue.

Ask:

> “Totally fair. Before you go, help me understand: is it the vehicle, the numbers, the trade, the timing, or something else that does not feel right yet?”

Then confirm:

> “So if we solve that part, are we still in the right conversation?”

### 5. Schedule the next move
No real opportunity should leave with vague follow-up.

Before the guest leaves, assign:
- the next action
- the owner
- the timing
- the reason for the follow-up

## Save-A-Deal Logic
When a guest does not buy, the question is not only, “Did we close them?”

The better question is:

> “What stopped the deal today, and what action gives us the best chance to keep it alive?”

### Common deal risks and next best actions

| Deal Risk | What It Usually Means | Manager Next Best Action |
|---|---|---|
| Payment concern | Guest may like the vehicle but not the structure | Build an affordability path: down payment, term, lease, alternative vehicle, or protection value |
| Trade concern | Guest may not understand equity, payoff, or market value | Revisit the appraisal story and explain the unshakable facts clearly |
| Vehicle concern | Right guest, wrong unit or unclear fit | Present one better-fit alternative and explain why |
| Spouse/decision-maker absent | The real decision is incomplete | Schedule a second visit or send a clean recap to share |
| Shopping competitor | Guest is comparing without full context | Clarify value, ownership experience, warranty, availability, and total deal structure |
| Timing issue | Guest may be real but not urgent | Create a specific future touchpoint tied to their timeline |
| Trust gap | Guest does not feel safe enough to move forward | Slow down, clarify facts, and remove pressure |

---

## The five questions every manager must answer
Before an unsold showroom guest leaves, the manager must be able to answer:

1. Who is the guest?
2. What vehicle are they here on?
3. Where are they in the process?
4. Why are they not moving forward today?
5. What is the next best action?

If we cannot answer those five questions, we did not manage the opportunity.

---

## Required same-day follow-up
Every unsold showroom guest with real opportunity must have same-day follow-up.

Minimum standard:
- salesperson text
- salesperson email
- salesperson phone call or voicemail
- manager text or call when appropriate
- CRM note with the real objection and next step
- Save-A-Deal summary when the opportunity is strong enough to protect

The follow-up should not sound like a generic “checking in.” It should connect to the guest’s actual concern.

Example:

> “Hi [Name], this is _____ at Clark Hyundai. I appreciated the time today. I know the main question was keeping the payment comfortable while still getting the right vehicle. I am going to work on a couple of clean options and follow up with you by [time].”

## Role-play lab
Use these drills in a 20- to 30-minute sales meeting.

### Drill 1: Busy showroom triage
**Setup:** Three guests are in the showroom. One salesperson is stuck, one guest is waiting, and one guest is close to leaving.

**Manager goal:** Identify the highest-risk opportunity and enter correctly.

Score the manager on:
- awareness
- speed
- calmness
- support of salesperson
- clarity of next step

---

### Drill 2: “We need to think about it”
**Guest line:** “We need to think about it.”

**Manager goal:** Find out whether the issue is vehicle, numbers, trade, timing, or confidence.

Required manager question:

> “Totally fair. When people say that, it is usually one of a few things: the vehicle, the numbers, the trade, the timing, or just needing more confidence. Which one is it for you?”

---

### Drill 3: Salesperson stuck, manager support
**Setup:** The salesperson is doing their best, but the guest is drifting and the deal is losing energy.

**Manager goal:** Step in without embarrassing the salesperson.

Manager entry:

> “You are in good hands with _____. I just wanted to jump in for a minute and make sure we are lining this up around what matters most to you.”

---

### Drill 4: Exit prevention
**Setup:** Guest is standing up and preparing to leave.

**Manager goal:** Preserve trust and create a real next step.

Manager line:

> “Before you head out, I do not want to pressure you. I just want to make sure we understand what would need to be different for this to make sense.”

---

### Drill 5: Save-A-Deal follow-up plan
**Setup:** The guest has left unsold.

**Manager goal:** Write the CRM note, assign the follow-up owner, and create the next best action.

The manager must complete:
- real objection
- exact guest words
- next best action
- follow-up time
- owner

---

## Coaching message for the team
Use this message to open the meeting:

> “This weekend showed us where we have a gap. We had showroom traffic, but our manager involvement and deal-saving rhythm were not where they need to be. This is not about blame. This is about protecting opportunities. When guests are in the showroom, managers must be active in the flow. We need to know who is here, where they are in the process, what is stopping the deal, and what the next best action is. Starting today, no real showroom opportunity leaves without manager awareness, manager involvement, and a documented follow-up plan.”

---

## Manager accountability standard
At the end of each active showroom opportunity, the sales manager should be able to say:

> “Here is what happened, here is why they left, here is the next move, here is who owns it, and here is when the follow-up happens.”

If the manager cannot say that, the opportunity was not managed.

---

## Daily reinforcement
For the next seven days, review every unsold showroom opportunity at the end of the day.

Ask:
- Did the manager touch the guest?
- Was the real objection documented?
- Was there a next best action?
- Was follow-up assigned?
- Did the CRM reflect the truth?

This is how the process becomes habit.
